Growth Mindset 2.0 for Leadership Teams

A deeper learning module for leadership teams across the organization. The focus is not growth mindset as a slogan or a set of beliefs. The focus is how leadership teams behave when priorities shift, uncertainty rises, and it becomes harder to stay open, aligned, and learning oriented.

This version is structured around Foundation, Implementation, and Scaling. Each class uses a realistic situation, interpretation, a short assessment, team discussion, and an action step with the aim of helping the team leave with something concrete.

How to use this module

This can be used by a leadership team together, by an internal facilitator, or by a leader preparing a team session. The module is designed to be practical rather than academic. It assumes you are dealing with real deadlines, competing priorities, and the normal friction that comes with leading across functions and through change.

A useful rhythm is to spend 25 to 35 minutes on each class. Read the situation together, discuss where you recognize yourselves, complete the quick assessment, and finish with one concrete action. The final section will help you pull the actions together into a simple team action plan.

Foundation • Class 1

What change readiness actually looks like in real work

Situation

Your leadership team has spent time building a good foundation. People know the language of growth mindset, feedback, learning, and collaboration. On paper, the team looks strong. Then a new set of priorities comes in. Timelines tighten. More coordination is needed across functions. A few leaders are pulled in different directions by their own teams and stakeholders.

In the next leadership meeting, the team still talks about staying open and learning oriented. At the same time, there is less patience for exploration. Updates become more transactional. Some team members speak less. Others speak more forcefully. It becomes harder to separate what is urgent from what is important, and harder to know whether the team is still working from shared understanding or just trying to keep momentum.

What typically happens

This is where many teams discover that change readiness is not the same as having the right concepts in place. It becomes visible in the quality of the interaction when pressure increases. A team may genuinely believe it is collaborative and learning oriented, while behaving in much narrower ways once the demands of the work increase.

Growth Mindset 2.0 is useful here because it shifts the focus away from identity and toward behavior. The question is not whether the team believes in growth mindset. The question is whether it can keep acting in growth oriented ways when it would be easier to close down, simplify too quickly, or retreat into function specific thinking.

Guiding principle

Change readiness is less about what the team says it values and more about what the team is able to do together when the work becomes demanding.

Quick assessment

When your leadership team is under pressure and priorities shift, which description is closest to your reality?

Team discussion

  • What changes first in our behavior when pressure increases?
  • Where do we still act in ways that reflect our intentions, and where do we drift away from them?
  • What do we tend to protect when change makes us uncomfortable: speed, harmony, certainty, or something else?

Action

Agree on one thing you want to pay more attention to in the next month.

Foundation • Class 2

From individual mindset to collective behavior

Situation

A leadership team is filled with capable people who individually see themselves as open minded and development oriented. Several members enjoy learning, ask thoughtful questions, and want their teams to experiment. Still, the collective dynamic is uneven. Some meetings are rich and useful. Others feel polite, rushed, and strangely narrow.

The issue is not that the individual leaders lack intent. The issue is that a team dynamic has its own life. A few dominant voices shape the tempo. A couple of members hold back until they feel sure their view will land well. Some topics are repeatedly framed from functional positions rather than from the broader needs of the organization. As a result, the team can have strong individuals and still produce weak collective learning.

What typically happens

This is one of the most important shifts in Growth Mindset 2.0. Development does not stop at the individual. Leadership happens in interaction. A team can only become more change ready if the way people work together changes, not just the language individuals use about themselves.

This is also why many organizations struggle. They invest in individual learning while the real bottleneck sits in team dynamics. If the team does not make room for challenge, shared reflection, and honest interpretation of what is happening, the learning remains private and fragmented.

Guiding principle

Strong individual intentions do not automatically create strong collective behavior. The team has to practice that together.

Quick assessment

When you look at your leadership team, which statement feels most accurate?

Team discussion

  • Where is our team dynamic stronger than the sum of our individual strengths?
  • Where does the collective behavior become weaker than what each of us would want?
  • What team habit would make it easier for us to think better together?

Action

Define one team level habit you want to strengthen.

Implementation • Class 3

Behavior under pressure and uncertainty

Situation

Your leadership team is responsible for a cross functional initiative that has been running for a few months. The ambition is clear, but progress has been slower than expected. Some teams are moving, others are stuck, and there is increasing pressure from above to show results.

In the meeting, people bring updates, but they are not fully aligned. One leader believes the issue is execution. Another believes the direction is still too unclear. A third sees it as a resource problem. Everyone is partly right, but no one is fully aligned. The discussion becomes careful. People choose their words. Disagreement is hinted at rather than explored.

After the meeting, some team members feel that important concerns were left unsaid. Others feel that the team did the right thing by moving forward. On the surface, the team looks aligned. Underneath, there are different interpretations of both the problem and the solution.

What typically happens

Most leadership teams do not struggle because they lack competence. They struggle because pressure changes how they behave together. Conversations become more cautious. Disagreement is softened. Decisions are made faster, but with less shared understanding.

Many teams believe they are open and learning oriented. In practice, those behaviors become harder to maintain when the work becomes demanding. Growth Mindset 2.0 is useful because it asks whether the team can keep learning when it would be easier to close down.

Guiding principle

Change readiness is not defined by what the team believes when things are easy. It is defined by how the team behaves when pressure makes it uncomfortable to stay open.

Quick assessment

When pressure increases and there are different views in the room, which pattern is closest to your team?

Team discussion

  • Where do we recognize ourselves in the patterns above?
  • What was left unsaid the last time we felt real pressure?
  • What would have been difficult but valuable to address more directly?

Action

Agree on one observable behavior you will strengthen in the next high pressure situation.

Implementation • Class 4

Feedback, mistakes, and learning loops

Situation

A decision made by the leadership team creates unexpected problems for one of the functions involved. The issue is not catastrophic, but it is visible and awkward. The people affected are frustrated. A few members of the leadership team feel that the warning signs were present earlier, but not addressed clearly enough.

In the next meeting, the mistake is mentioned, but only briefly. The conversation moves quickly to damage control and next steps. This feels productive, yet something important is missed. The team does not spend enough time understanding how the decision was made, what signals were ignored, or why feedback was not used well enough at the time.

What typically happens

Many leadership teams say they want learning cultures, but when a mistake is close to their own decision making the response often narrows. Teams either move too quickly to solutions or become slightly defensive. Neither response creates a strong learning loop.

Growth Mindset 2.0 is relevant here because it pushes beyond “mistakes are okay” as a slogan. The deeper issue is whether the team can examine mistakes without blame, without avoidance, and without immediately protecting its own image. Mature learning comes from understanding what happened, not simply recovering from it.

Guiding principle

Learning does not improve just because a team is willing to talk about mistakes. It improves when the team becomes better at examining how those mistakes emerged.

Quick assessment

When something goes wrong that reflects back on your leadership team, what usually happens?

Team discussion

  • How does our team usually respond when an issue reflects back on our own choices?
  • What do we tend to skip too quickly when we review a setback?
  • What would strengthen the quality of feedback and learning inside this team?

Action

Define one learning loop you want to strengthen after mistakes or setbacks.

Implementation • Class 5

Cross-functional collaboration when priorities compete

Situation

Your leadership team is trying to move a strategic priority that requires multiple functions to work together. Everyone agrees that the priority matters. At the same time, each leader is carrying strong demands from their own area. The commercial side wants speed. Operations wants feasibility. People teams want sustainability and clarity for the organization. Finance is asking for discipline and focus.

The tension is not caused by bad intent. It comes from the fact that each perspective contains something valid. The problem is that when the pressure rises, those perspectives can harden into positions. The leadership team starts to defend its own pieces of reality instead of jointly making sense of the whole. Collaboration becomes more political, even if nobody would describe it that way.

What typically happens

This is one of the clearest tests of change readiness. Many teams collaborate well when interests are broadly aligned. The real challenge comes when collaboration requires people to hold competing truths at the same time and still work toward a shared direction.

Growth Mindset 2.0 is useful here because it asks whether the team can stay curious enough to understand the other perspective without losing the discipline to make decisions. Teams that scale well across boundaries are not free of tension. They are better at working with it.

Guiding principle

Cross-functional collaboration becomes stronger when leaders move beyond defending their function and take joint ownership of the wider problem.

Quick assessment

When priorities compete across functions, what usually happens in your leadership team?

Team discussion

  • Where does collaboration across our functions feel strongest, and where does it become more transactional?
  • What kinds of tensions do we avoid naming directly?
  • What would help us move from compromise to stronger joint ownership?

Action

Choose one way to strengthen cross-functional ownership in the next important initiative.

Scaling • Class 6

From leadership team insight to team-wide practice

Situation

Your leadership team has had a useful conversation. You have identified patterns, named a few uncomfortable truths, and agreed on some practical actions. The immediate risk is feeling satisfied too early. The insight stays at leadership team level, while the wider organization continues to experience the same old habits.

This happens often. Leadership teams do good reflective work, but do not translate it into routines, language, and expectations that others can feel. The team talks about change readiness in its own sessions, but the teams below still experience mixed signals, unclear priorities, or too little space for challenge and learning.

What typically happens

Scaling is where many development efforts lose strength. The issue is rarely that the insight was wrong. The issue is that it never became part of how the leadership team leads. If the team does not model the behaviors, create simple structures, and make the expectations visible to others, very little changes beyond the room.

Growth Mindset 2.0 becomes much more powerful when it is treated as a leadership practice rather than a learning theme. That means the leadership team asks not only what it should improve, but also how the people it leads will notice that it is serious about this.

Guiding principle

Leadership team learning becomes meaningful when it changes how the team leads others, not just how it talks to itself.

Quick assessment

When your leadership team has a useful insight, what usually happens next?

Team discussion

  • How would the teams we lead describe our consistency on these topics today?
  • What would they need to see, hear, or experience differently from us?
  • What one routine, message, or expectation could we make more visible in the next month?

Action

Define one action that helps your leadership team’s learning trickle down into the teams you lead.
