Growth Mindset 2.0 for Leadership Teams

A tool-driven learning session for leadership teams. The purpose is not to explain growth mindset again. The purpose is to help a team see how change readiness shows up in real situations, where the gaps are, and what needs to change in practice.

This version is built more like a guided working session than a traditional course. The interaction is varied on purpose. Some parts challenge assumptions. Some create a clearer picture of current reality. Others help the team work with real situations and leave with specific next steps.

Part 1

Reset the perspective

This is where many leadership teams get stuck

Most leadership teams are already working with the right words. Growth mindset, feedback, learning, collaboration, psychological safety. In many organizations, those ideas have been around for years. They are part of leadership development, part of internal language, and often part of what teams say they want more of.

The issue is rarely awareness. It is implementation under pressure.

When priorities shift, when execution starts to slip, when cross functional tension rises, or when something goes wrong, the quality of interaction in the room changes. Conversations become narrower. Some people become more careful. Others push harder for speed or certainty. Teams that describe themselves as open and learning oriented can become much less so in the moments where learning would matter most.

That is why Growth Mindset 2.0 is not really about whether leaders like the concept of growth mindset. It is about whether a leadership team can operate in a learning oriented way when the context makes that harder.

Working principle

This is less about what your team says it values and more about what your team is able to do together when the work becomes demanding.

Quick positioning

Which statement is closest to your current reality?

Team reflection

  • Where do we see the biggest gap between what we believe in and how we actually operate?
  • When the stakes rise, what tends to happen to the quality of our conversations?
  • What topics become harder to address directly when the team is under strain?

Part 3

Expand the Comfort Zone

Navigate the mindset zones

Growth mindset becomes much more practical when you stop treating it as a fixed quality and start treating it as a response to a situation. Leaders and teams move between openness, caution, defensiveness, learning, and action depending on what is happening around them.

The mindset zones help make that visible. The comfort zone is where performance is familiar and relatively stable. The fear zone is where uncertainty increases and reactions such as hesitation, defensiveness, control, or silence begin to show up. The learning zone is where curiosity, challenge, and adjustment become possible. The growth zone is where new capability starts to stick.

The point is not to label people. It is to help a leadership team recognize where it is in a real situation and what would help it move forward.

ComfortKnown patterns. Stable performance. Low stretch.
FearHesitation, defensiveness, control, silence, narrowed thinking.
LearningQuestions, openness, challenge, experimentation, adjustment.
GrowthNew capability becoming repeatable and integrated.

Zone reflection

Think of a recent leadership situation involving uncertainty, pressure, or cross-functional tension. Where was your team mostly operating?

Action

Identify one signal that tells you the team is moving into fear and one behavior that could help the team stay in learning for longer.

Part 4

Leadership situations

The point here is not to find the perfect answer. It is to see what your team tends to do in situations where change readiness is tested.

Situation 1 — Pressure and disagreement

Your leadership team is responsible for a cross-functional initiative that is falling behind. One leader sees the issue as execution. Another sees it as unclear direction. A third believes the organization is overloaded. The meeting moves on, but not all perspectives are explored equally, and the pressure to decide is growing.

Situation 2 — A mistake reflects back on leadership

A decision made by the leadership team leads to visible problems for part of the organization. The issue is manageable, but uncomfortable. A few people feel the warning signs were visible earlier. In the next meeting, the team can either move quickly to solutions or slow down to understand how the issue emerged.

What do you notice?

  • Do your answers optimize more for speed, harmony, or learning?
  • Which of these situations feels most familiar right now?
  • What do your choices suggest about your team’s current change readiness?
