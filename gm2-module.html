What this module does

This module helps leadership teams move beyond growth mindset as a concept and work with it as a practical capability. The focus is not just on beliefs or intent, but on how leaders and teams behave under pressure, uncertainty, cross-functional tension, feedback moments, and change.

How it works

Each class includes four parts. First, a short insight section. Second, a mini assessment. Third, an exercise for individual or team use. Fourth, reflection prompts to help turn insight into action. Your notes stay saved in your browser unless you export them.

Class 1

Growth mindset as change readiness in real work

Insights

Most leadership teams understand growth mindset in theory. They know learning matters. They talk about feedback, curiosity, experimentation, and learning from mistakes. Yet when pressure rises, a different pattern often appears. Teams move faster to certainty, defend existing views, narrow participation, and become more cautious in how they learn.

This is why Growth Mindset 2.0 is better understood as change readiness. It is about how a leadership team responds when the environment is shifting and the right answer is not yet obvious. The real question is not whether the team likes the idea of learning. It is whether the team can keep learning when real work becomes demanding.

Mini assessment

Exercise

Pick one recent leadership team situation where change, uncertainty, or cross-functional tension was present. Describe what happened, how the team responded, and what this says about your current change readiness.

Reflection

  • Where do we still treat growth mindset more as a value than a visible behavior?
  • What kinds of situations make us more cautious, defensive, or closed?
  • Where are we stronger than we think in how we respond to change?
Class 2

Leadership behavior under pressure and uncertainty

Insights

Growth mindset becomes visible in behavior. The strongest signals are often found in small moments. How a leader reacts to a challenge. How quickly a team moves from blame to learning. Whether disagreement is explored or closed down. Whether feedback is used to strengthen a decision or treated as friction.

Under pressure, leadership teams often default to habits that feel efficient in the moment, but limit learning. They may over-control, reduce dialogue, protect assumptions, or avoid naming difficult realities. A practical growth mindset asks a harder question. What does this team actually do when the pressure is on?

Mini assessment

Exercise

Think of a moment where your leadership team was under pressure. Map what happened across three stages: what triggered the pressure, what behaviors showed up, and what the consequences were for team learning and alignment.

Reflection

  • What patterns show up most often when we feel time pressure or uncertainty?
  • Where do our leadership intentions and actual behavior differ most?
  • What is one behavior we should strengthen in the next month?
Class 3

Make it work as a team: habits, structure, and follow-through

Insights

Leadership teams do not become more change ready through intention alone. They need structures that support the right behaviors repeatedly. This may include stronger reflection habits, clearer meeting routines, better follow-up on experiments, more deliberate use of feedback, and clearer language for how the team wants to respond to change.

This is where a leadership team moves from insight to capability. The goal is not a perfect culture. It is a more consistent practice. That is also why simple tools matter. They help a team pause, assess, and act in ways that are easier to repeat.

Mini assessment

Exercise

Define one simple team habit for the next 30 days. It should support stronger change readiness in real work. Keep it concrete, light, and easy to repeat.

Reflection

  • What is most likely to stop us from following through?
  • How can we make growth mindset more visible in our weekly leadership rhythm?
  • What would stronger change readiness look like in practice three months from now?
